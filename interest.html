Simple vs Compound Interest

Two fundamentally different ways to calculate interest — and choosing the wrong one in an exam can cost you the entire problem. Simple interest applies the interest rate only to the original principal, yielding a linear growth curve. Compound interest applies the rate to the accumulated total each period, producing exponential growth.

In engineering practice, compound interest is almost universally used unless explicitly stated otherwise. Banks, loans, and investment analyses all assume compounding.

// SIMPLE INTEREST — linear growth F(n) = P(1 + n·i) Eq. 1 Example: ₦1,000 @ 10% for 5 yrs: F = 1000(1 + 5×0.10) = 1000×1.5 = ₦1,500 // COMPOUND INTEREST — exponential growth F(n) = P(1 + i)ⁿ Eq. 2 Example: ₦1,000 @ 10% for 5 yrs: F = 1000(1.10)⁵ = 1000×1.6105 = ₦1,610.51 // Difference = ₦110.51 → 7.4% more

The gap widens dramatically over time — this is the power (and danger) of compound interest.

Derivation of Compound Formula
F(1) = P + P·i = P(1+i) F(2) = F(1)(1+i) = P(1+i)² F(3) = F(2)(1+i) = P(1+i)³ ⋮ F(n) = P(1+i)ⁿ ← General case

Each year's principal = prior year's total, so interest accumulates on the growing balance.

The 6 Core Interest Factors

Your Problem-Solving Toolkit

Every engineering economics calculation reduces to one (or a combination) of these six factor equations. The notation (X/Y, i%, n) means "find X given Y, at interest rate i%, over n periods." The factor value is read from the compound interest tables.

THE SIX STANDARD INTEREST FACTORS — From Prof. K.C. Bala's GET 311 Notes
# Factor Name Notation Formula Finds Given Use Case
1 Compound Amount (F/P, i%, n) (1+i)ⁿ F P Future value of a present sum
2 Present Worth (P/F, i%, n) (1+i)⁻ⁿ P F Discount a future sum to today
3 Series Compound Amount (F/A, i%, n) [(1+i)ⁿ−1]/i F A Future worth of regular payments
4 Sinking Fund (A/F, i%, n) i/[(1+i)ⁿ−1] A F Regular deposit to reach target F
5 Series Present Worth (P/A, i%, n) [(1+i)ⁿ−1]/[i(1+i)ⁿ] P A PW of a series of equal payments
6 Capital Recovery (A/P, i%, n) i(1+i)ⁿ/[(1+i)ⁿ−1] A P Loan repayment calculation
Memory Aid — Reading the Notation

(F/P, 8%, 10) means:
"Find F, given P, at 8% for 10 periods." The slash literally reads "given." Numerator is what you want; denominator is what you have.

Factor pairs are reciprocals: (F/P)×(P/F) = 1. So if you know one, you can invert for the other. (A/F) and (F/A) are also reciprocals.

Notice: (A/F) + i = (A/P). The capital recovery factor equals the sinking fund factor plus the interest rate. This relationship helps you double-check table values.

Effective vs Nominal Interest Rate

When a bank quotes "18% compounded monthly," the 18% is the nominal rate — the stated annual rate that does not account for within-year compounding. The effective rate is the true annual rate that produces the same result. It is always ≥ the nominal rate.

For engineering problems, always convert to the effective rate before using annual interest factor tables, unless the problem specifies the compounding period matches the payment period.

// Effective Annual Rate i_eff = (1 + r/m)ᵐ − 1 Eq. 18 where: r = nominal annual rate m = compounding periods/year // Example: r=18%, monthly (m=12) i_eff = (1 + 0.18/12)¹² − 1 = (1.015)¹² − 1 = 1.1956 − 1 = 19.56%

Past Paper Example — Q38: 18% Compounded Daily

What effective rate corresponds to 18% compounded daily? (1 year = 360 days)

i_eff = (1 + 0.18/360)³⁶⁰ − 1 = (1.0005)³⁶⁰ − 1 // (1.0005)³⁶⁰ ≈ e^(0.18) ≈ 1.19722 = 1.19722 − 1 = 19.72% ← Answer D in Q38
⚑ Common Pitfalls — Interest Formulae
  • Wrong formula for given/find: If you need to find A given P, use (A/P) — Capital Recovery Factor. Students often mistakenly use (A/F) — Sinking Fund. Remember: the denominator tells you what you already know.
  • Forgetting to convert % to decimal: i = 10% means i = 0.10 in all formulas. Using i = 10 gives a wildly wrong answer.
  • Misidentifying compounding period: If the problem says "12% compounded quarterly," the quarterly rate is 3% and n is in quarters, not i = 12% and n in years.
  • Inverting factors incorrectly: (P/F, 8%, 5) = 0.6806. So (F/P, 8%, 5) = 1/0.6806 = 1.4693 — always verify with the full formula.
Self-Assessment · Interest & Formulae
Q1. What is the factor name for the formula i/[(1+i)ⁿ − 1]?
A. Capital Recovery Factor (A/P)
B. Series Present Worth Factor (P/A)
C. Sinking Fund Factor (A/F)
D. Compound Amount Factor (F/P)
✓ Answer: C — Sinking Fund Factor (A/F, i%, n).
This factor converts a future sum F into the equivalent uniform annual deposit A. Compare with Capital Recovery (A/P) which uses i(1+i)ⁿ in the numerator. The Sinking Fund Factor is the answer to Q113/Q114 in the past paper.
Q2. A loan of ₦50,000 to be repaid in 3 years as ₦65,000. What is the effective annual interest rate?
A. 9.01%
B. 9.14%
C. 9.31%
D. 9.41%
✓ Answer: B — 9.14%.
Using F = P(1+i)ⁿ: 65,000 = 50,000(1+i)³
(1+i)³ = 1.3 → (1+i) = 1.3^(1/3) = 1.0914 → i = 9.14%. This is Q37 in the past paper.
Q3. At what annual rate will ₦1,000 triple in 10 years (compound interest)?
A. 10.00%
B. 11.61%
C. 12.50%
D. 15.00%
✓ Answer: B — 11.61%.
3000 = 1000(1+i)¹⁰ → (1+i)¹⁰ = 3 → (1+i) = 3^(0.1) = 1.1161 → i = 11.61%. This is the exercise from the GET 311 lecture notes: "What interest rate triples ₦1,000 in 10 years?"

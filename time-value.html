Why Money Has a Time Dimension

The concept of the Time Value of Money (TVM) is deceptively simple yet profoundly powerful. It states that an amount of money available at the present time is worth more than the identical amount at any future date, given a positive rate of interest. This is not merely an economic abstraction — it is a mathematical fact rooted in money's capacity to earn returns.

Consider a straightforward example from Prof. Bala's notes: if you deposit ₦200 in a bank at 10% per year, you earn ₦20 in interest by year-end, giving you ₦220. This means ₦220 receivable one year from today is exactly equivalent to ₦200 today — at that interest rate. The ₦20 gap is the quantified time value.

But why does this gap exist at all? There are four interconnected reasons, and understanding all of them helps you reason about any TVM problem from first principles rather than just applying formulas blindly.

The Four Pillars of TVM

① Earning Power of Capital

Money is productive. Invested capital generates returns — through interest, dividends, or business profit. A sum available now can be immediately deployed to earn more money. One naira today can become 1.1 naira in one year; a future promise of ₦1 has no such guarantees.

② Inflation — The Silent Thief

In an inflationary economy, the general price level rises over time, meaning each naira buys progressively fewer goods and services. ₦5,000 that bought a specific quantity of materials in 2020 buys less of the same materials in 2025. The purchasing power of future money is eroded.

③ Risk and Uncertainty

Future cash flows are never certain. The debtor might default; the project might fail; economic conditions might shift. A definite sum today carries zero risk of non-receipt. A promised future sum carries all the uncertainty of the intervening period. Rational agents demand a risk premium for accepting future money over present money.

④ Preference for Present Consumption

Human psychology exhibits a general preference for having goods and services now rather than later. This is sometimes called "time preference" or "impatience." Even without inflation or risk, people must be compensated (with interest) to defer consumption to the future.

Standard Notation
P = Present Worth (₦) = Principal amount F = Future Worth (₦) = Value at end of n n = Number of periods = Years/months/etc. i = Interest rate/period = Expressed as decimal A = Equal end-of-period = Annuity payment (₦) G = Uniform gradient = Increase per period

These six variables appear in virtually every engineering economics problem. Master their definitions before anything else.

Quick Equivalence Check

At 10% annual interest, these amounts are equivalent:

Today (t=0): ₦1,000 Year 1: ₦1,100 Year 2: ₦1,210 Year 3: ₦1,331 Year 5: ₦1,610.51

Any one of these can be exchanged for another at 10% — that is the meaning of equivalence.

"₦1,000 today is worth more than ₦1,000 in the future — but how much more? Precisely as much as it would earn between now and then."
— TVM Principle
Worked Examples

TVM in Action

Example 1 — The Bank Deposit

₦200 deposited at 10%/year. What is the value after 1 year? After 5 years (compound)?

// After 1 year Interest = 200 × 0.10 = ₦20 Total = 200 + 20 = ₦220 // After 5 years (compound) F = 200(1 + 0.10)⁵ = 200 × 1.6105 = ₦322.10 // After 5 years (simple — for comparison) F = 200(1 + 5×0.10) = 200 × 1.5 = ₦300.00 Difference = ₦22.10 (7.4% more with compound)

The ₦22.10 difference shows why compound interest grows faster — you earn interest on your interest.

Example 2 — Equivalence Decision

Would you prefer ₦1,000 today or ₦1,200 in 3 years if the bank rate is 15%?

// Grow ₦1,000 forward 3 years @ 15% F = 1000(1.15)³ = 1000 × 1.5209 = ₦1,520.88 // Compare: ₦1,520.88 vs ₦1,200 ₦1,520.88 > ₦1,200 ✓ ∴ Take ₦1,000 TODAY // Alternative: bring ₦1,200 back to today P = 1200 / (1.15)³ = 1200 / 1.5209 = ₦788.86 ₦1,000 > ₦788.86 ← Same conclusion

You can either push P forward or pull F back — the decision is the same either way.

The Concept of Equivalence

Equivalence is what makes economic comparison possible. Two cash flows are equivalent when they have the same economic value at a given interest rate, even if they differ in timing and amount.

This is the entire basis for comparing engineering alternatives. You cannot directly compare ₦500,000 spent now on Machine A against ₦80,000/year operating costs for Machine B over 10 years — they occur at different times. Equivalence lets you bring all costs to the same moment in time (usually the present), making comparison valid.

The key insight: if two cash flows are equivalent, you should be indifferent between them. An investor earning 10%/year should be genuinely indifferent between receiving ₦1,000 today and ₦1,100 one year from now.

// Equivalence at 10%/year These are ALL equivalent: t=0: ₦1,000.00 (present) t=1: ₦1,100.00 (×1.1) t=2: ₦1,210.00 (×1.1²) t=3: ₦1,331.00 (×1.1³) // You can move any cash flow // forward (×(1+i)ⁿ) or backward // (÷(1+i)ⁿ) freely
Important Note

Equivalence changes with the interest rate. ₦1,000 and ₦1,100 are equivalent at 10%, but NOT at 8% (where ₦1,000 grows to only ₦1,080). Always specify the interest rate when discussing equivalence.

⚑ Common Pitfalls — Avoid These in the Exam
  • Confusing nominal and effective rates: A bank quoting "18% compounded monthly" does NOT mean 18% per year if you compute it as a monthly rate. The effective annual rate is (1 + 0.18/12)¹² − 1 = 19.56%. Always convert.
  • Ignoring compounding frequency: "12% compounded quarterly" means i = 3% per quarter, n measured in quarters. Students often plug in i = 12% and n in years — wrong!
  • Comparing money at different times directly: You cannot say "I received ₦2,000 in Year 1 and spent ₦3,000 in Year 5" and conclude a ₦1,000 net cost without adjusting for time.
  • Forgetting the direction: P→F is compounding (multiply by (1+i)ⁿ). F→P is discounting (divide by (1+i)ⁿ). These are inverses, not the same operation.
  • Assuming inflation = interest rate: The nominal interest rate includes both inflation compensation AND a real return. Real rate ≈ nominal rate − inflation rate (Fisher equation).
Self-Assessment · Time Value of Money
Q1. The time value of money concept states that, given a positive interest rate:
A. A future sum is always preferable to the same sum today
B. A present sum is worth more than the same sum available in the future
C. Money has no intrinsic value — only goods have value
D. Inflation and interest rate are always equal
✓ Answer: B. A present sum is worth more than the same sum in the future, because present money can be invested to earn returns. This is the core TVM statement. Options A and C directly contradict it; D is false — inflation and interest rate are related but not equal.
Q2. Which of the following is NOT a reason for the time value of money?
A. Money can earn interest when invested
B. Inflation erodes purchasing power over time
C. The colour and design of currency notes changes periodically
D. Future cash flows carry uncertainty and risk
✓ Answer: C. Currency redesign is irrelevant to economic value. The three genuine reasons are: (1) earning power, (2) inflation, and (3) risk/uncertainty. These constitute the "time preference for money" concept in engineering economics.
Q3. ₦500 is deposited today at 20% per year, compounded annually. What is the accumulated value after 2 years?
A. ₦700 (simple interest result)
B. ₦720 (compound interest result)
C. ₦750
D. ₦660
✓ Answer: B — ₦720.
F = 500 × (1.20)² = 500 × 1.44 = ₦720

Common trap: Option A (₦700) is the simple interest answer: F = 500(1 + 2×0.2) = 500×1.4 = ₦700. If the question says "compounded," always use F = P(1+i)ⁿ, not P(1+ni).

Depreciation vs Depletion

Assets that generate economic value over multiple years gradually lose that value through use, obsolescence, or physical decay. Depreciation accounts for this loss systematically, spreading the asset's cost over its useful life as an annual expense charge.

Depletion is the analogous concept for natural resources — oil wells, mines, timber stands, quarries. As the resource is extracted, its value diminishes. The two depletion methods are the Unit (Factor) Method and the Percentage Method.

Key Definitions
P = First Cost (purchase + install + taxes) S = Salvage Value (estimated resale at end) n = Useful Life (years) BV = Book Value at any time k d = Annual depreciation amount
Types of Depreciation (Q88, Q89)
  • 📉
    Physical Depreciation: Due to wear, tear, and reduced ability to produce. The machine breaks down more often, requires more maintenance, outputs less.
  • 🔄
    Functional (Obsolescence) Depreciation: Demand for the function the asset performs decreases, or a superior technology replaces it. A functional machine may be abandoned because something better exists.
"Depreciation is not a cash expense — it is an accounting allocation of a past cash outflow over time."
— Engineering Economics Principle

The Four Depreciation Methods
Method 1: Straight-Line (SL) — Simplest, Most Common

Assumes loss in value is directly proportional to age. Every year depreciates the asset by the same fixed amount. The book value declines linearly from P to S over n years.

Annual depreciation: d = (P − S) / n Book Value at year k: BV_k = P − k·d = P − k·(P−S)/n // Q59: P=₦8,000, S=₦500, n=10 d = (8000−500)/10 = ₦750/year BV₈ = 8000 − 8×750 = ₦2,000 ← Answer A
SL Characteristics
  • ✓
    Simplest to compute
  • ✓
    Equal tax deductions each year
  • ✗
    May not reflect true value loss
  • ✗
    No time value consideration
Method 2: Sum-of-Years Digits (SYD) — Accelerated

Depreciates more heavily in early years, tapering off over time. This better reflects the reality that new assets lose value faster. Favoured for tax purposes.

SOYD = n(n+1)/2 Depreciation in year k: d_k = (n−k+1)/SOYD × (P−S) // Q9/Q61: P=₦9,000, S=₦1,000, n=10 SOYD = 10×11/2 = 55 d₁ = (10/55)(8000) = ₦1,454.55 d₂ = (9/55)(8000) = ₦1,309.09 d₃ = (8/55)(8000) = ₦1,163.64 Sum (3 yrs) = ₦3,927.27 ← Answer B
SYD — The NUMERATOR Pattern

For n=10:

Year 1: numerator = 10 Year 2: numerator = 9 Year 3: numerator = 8 ⋮ Year 10: numerator = 1 SOYD (denominator) = 55

Trap: Year k numerator = n−k+1 (NOT n−k). At year 1 it must equal n.

Method 3: Declining Balance (DB) — Fixed % of Book Value

Annual depreciation is a fixed percentage of the remaining book value at the start of that year. Depreciation amount declines each year (hence the name). Often used as Double Declining Balance (DDB) with d% = 2/n.

Rate: d% = 1 − (S/P)^(1/n) // OR fixed rate like 2/n Book Value: BV_k = P(1 − d%)ᵏ Annual dep: d_k = d% × BV_{k-1} // Note: DB never mathematically reaches exactly S // Often switched to SL when SL gives larger deduction

The answer to Q87 is Declining Balance — "the method where annual depreciation cost is a fixed percentage of book value at the beginning of the year."

Method 4: Sinking Fund — Accumulate for Replacement

Establishes a hypothetical fund that grows at interest rate i, reaching (P−S) by end of life n. Annual deposit A is constant, but the depreciation charge (deposit + interest earned on fund) increases each year.

Annual deposit: A = (P−S)(A/F, i%, n) Depreciation charge in year k: d_k = A + i×(accumulated fund at end of yr k-1) // Initial depreciation is LOW — grows over time // This is why it's seldom used (Q112 answer = D) // Companies prefer HIGH early depreciation for tax

Depletion Methods

Unit Method (Factor Method) — Q8, Q95, Q96
Depletion charge = (Initial Cost × Units produced this yr) / Total estimated units in property // Q8: Saudi oil well = ($50,000,000 × 500,000) / 5,000,000 = $50M × 0.10 = $5,000,000 ← Answer A
Percentage Method (Depletion Allowance) — Q62, Q93, Q94
Depletion = Fixed % of Gross Income (Maximum = 50% of net taxable income) Oil: 22% of gross income // Q62: Shell, gross income ₦50M Depletion = 0.22 × 50M = ₦11M But max = 50% × net taxable ₦18.5M = ₦9.25M ∴ Allowable depletion = ₦9,250,000 ← Answer C
⚑ Common Pitfalls — Depreciation
  • SYD numerator error: In year k, numerator = (n−k+1). At year 1 = n; at year n = 1. Students write (n−k) — off by one throughout.
  • Depreciation vs Depletion: Depreciation applies to manufactured assets (machines, buildings). Depletion applies to natural resource extraction (oil, coal, timber). Don't mix them up.
  • DB never reaches salvage exactly: The formula BV_k = P(1−d%)ᵏ asymptotically approaches zero, not S. In practice, switch to SL when SL gives higher depreciation.
  • Sinking Fund: initial depreciation is LOW: Unlike SYD/DB, the sinking fund method gives small charges early and large charges later. This is the opposite of what businesses want for tax deferral. Q112 answer is D.
Self-Assessment · Depreciation
Q1. Asset purchased for ₦9,000, sold for ₦1,000 after 10 years (SYD method). Total depreciation in first 3 years?
A. ₦3,279.27
B. ₦3,927.27
C. ₦3,729.27
D. ₦3,792.72
✓ Answer: B — ₦3,927.27. SOYD=55, P−S=₦8,000. d₁=10/55×8000=₦1,454.55; d₂=9/55×8000=₦1,309.09; d₃=8/55×8000=₦1,163.64. Total = ₦3,927.27. This is Q9 and Q61 in the past paper.
Q2. Why is the sinking fund depreciation method seldom used in industry?
A. It is mathematically too complex
B. It requires government regulatory approval
C. It ignores the asset's salvage value
D. The initial depreciation charge is low
✓ Answer: D. The sinking fund produces low depreciation charges early and high charges later. Businesses prefer the opposite — high early depreciation (SYD, DB) to reduce taxable income sooner (tax deferral). This is Q112 in the past paper.
Q3. The Declining Balance method assumes that annual depreciation is:
A. Equal each year (constant amount)
B. Proportional to the remaining years of life
C. A fixed percentage of the book value at the start of the year
D. Based on an interest-bearing sinking fund
✓ Answer: C. DB applies a fixed % (e.g., 20% or 2/n for DDB) to the declining book value. Since BV decreases each year, the annual charge also decreases — hence "declining balance." This is Q87 in the past paper.

What Is a Cash Flow Diagram?

A cash flow diagram is a timeline — a horizontal axis representing periods (0, 1, 2, … n) with vertical arrows indicating the magnitude and direction of money movements. Upward arrows (↑) represent inflows: receipts, income, salvage values, revenues. Downward arrows (↓) represent outflows: costs, expenses, investments, loan repayments.

The power of the diagram is that it forces you to be precise about when each cash flow occurs. A ₦500,000 machine purchase is at t=0 (now); the salvage value is at t=n (end of life). Misplacing one arrow by a period is a common source of error.

The Five Diagram Rules
  • 1
    Horizontal axis = time. Marks at t = 0, 1, 2, … n.
  • 2
    Arrow UP (↑) = cash inflow (receive, earn, salvage).
  • 3
    Arrow DOWN (↓) = cash outflow (pay, invest, operate).
  • 4
    Arrow height is proportional to the amount.
  • 5
    Default convention: all flows at END of period. Period 0 = "now" (beginning of period 1).

Types of Cash Flow Patterns

① Single Payment

One lump sum at a specific time point. The simplest pattern — handled by (F/P) or (P/F) factors.

P at t=0 ←→ F at t=n F = P(F/P, i%, n) P = F(P/F, i%, n)
② Uniform Series (Annuity)

Equal amounts at equal intervals. Car loans, mortgage payments, equipment leases. Use (F/A), (P/A), (A/F), (A/P) factors.

│ A │ A │ A │ A │ A 0 1 2 3 4 n
③ Arithmetic Gradient

Payments that increase by a constant amount G each period. Maintenance costs often follow this pattern. Period 1 payment = 0 (gradient starts at period 2).

Period: 1 2 3 4 n Amount: 0 G 2G 3G … (n-1)G P = G(P/G, i%, n) A = G(A/G, i%, n) ← convert to annuity
④ Deferred Annuity

Payments start at a period D later than period 1. Common in loan structures where repayment is deferred.

P' = A(P/A, i%, n−D+1) ← at period D-1 P = P'(P/F, i%, D−1) ← discounted to t=0 Combined: P = A(P/A,i%,n−D+1)(P/F,i%,D−1)
Diagram Example from Lecture Notes

Cash flows over 5 periods (₦):

t=0: ↓ ₦400 (initial outflow) t=1: ↑ ₦350 t=2: ↑ ₦400 ↓ ₦300 → Net: +₦100 t=3: ↑ ₦600 ↓ ₦200 → Net: +₦400 t=4: ↑ ₦800 ↓ ₦600 → Net: +₦200 t=5: ↑ ₦700 ↓ ₦300 → Net: +₦400 Total Net Cash Flow = −400+350+100+400+200+400 = +₦1,050
Gradient Worked Example

Machine maintenance starts at ₦1,000 in year 2, increasing by ₦500/year. n=5 years, i=10%. Find equivalent annual cost A.

// Base annuity A₁ = ₦1,000 from yr 1 // Gradient G = ₦500 starting yr 2 A_gradient = G(A/G, 10%, 5) = 500 × 1.810 = ₦905 Total A = A₁ + A_gradient = 1,000 + 905 = ₦1,905
Perspective Matters!

The same transaction looks different from different viewpoints. A bank lending ₦100,000 has an outflow at t=0; the borrower has an inflow. Always define whose perspective you are drawing before starting. In most exam problems, you draw from the investor's or firm's perspective.

⚑ Common Pitfalls — Cash Flow Diagrams
  • End-of-period vs beginning-of-period: The standard assumption is end-of-period. If a machine is bought NOW (beginning of period 1), it goes at t=0, not t=1.
  • Counting periods incorrectly: A project from Jan 2020 to Dec 2025 spans 5 years (n=5), not 6. Always count intervals, not endpoints.
  • Gradient period numbering: In the standard gradient series, the payment at period 1 is ZERO. The gradient G first appears at period 2. Students often start with G at period 1.
  • Forgetting salvage value: The estimated resale value at end of life is a positive inflow at t=n. It reduces the effective cost of ownership and must appear on the diagram.
Self-Assessment · Cash Flow & Diagrams
Q1. In a standard cash flow diagram, an upward arrow represents:
A. A cost or disbursement (outflow)
B. A cash inflow, receipt, or revenue
C. An arithmetic gradient increment
D. The interest rate per period
✓ Answer: B — cash inflow. By universal convention, upward arrows = inflows (receipts, income, revenues, salvage values). Downward arrows = outflows (costs, expenses, investments). This convention is stated explicitly in Prof. Bala's Section 2.2.6.
Q2. In a standard arithmetic gradient series, what is the payment amount at period 1?
A. G (the gradient amount)
B. 2G
C. Zero (0)
D. (n−1)G
✓ Answer: C — Zero. The standard gradient series has payments: 0 at period 1, G at period 2, 2G at period 3, …, (n−1)G at period n. The gradient is often combined with a base annuity A₁ to represent maintenance costs that start at some level and increase each year.
Q3. A deferred annuity has 6 payments, with first payment at end of period 4. What is D (delay period)?
A. D = 3, last payment at period 8
B. D = 4, last payment at period 9
C. D = 4, last payment at period 6
D. D = 3, last payment at period 9
✓ Answer: B — D=4, last payment at period 9. D is the period when the first payment occurs = 4. With 6 payments, last payment = 4 + 6 − 1 = period 9. The formula reference point P' is at end of period D−1 = 3.

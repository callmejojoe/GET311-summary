Glossary of Terms
🔍
Annuity
A uniform series of equal payments at equal time intervals. The most common cash flow pattern in loans and savings problems.
P = A(P/A, i%, n) · F = A(F/A, i%, n)
Annuity Due
An annuity where payments occur at the BEGINNING of each period (contrast with ordinary annuity, where payments are at period end).
Value = Ordinary Annuity × (1+i)
Book Value (BV)
The recorded value of an asset at any given time, equal to first cost minus total accumulated depreciation to date.
BV_k = P − Total Depreciation to year k
Break-Even Point (BEP)
The output level where Total Revenue equals Total Cost. Neither profit nor loss is made. The minimum sales volume required to cover all costs.
Q_BEP = Fixed Cost / (Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit)
Capital Recovery Factor
Converts a present sum P into a uniform series A over n periods. Used to determine loan repayment instalments.
(A/P, i%, n) = i(1+i)ⁿ / [(1+i)ⁿ−1]
Capitalized Cost
The present worth of all costs for a project over an infinite (perpetual) time horizon. Used for infrastructure with indefinite life.
CC = First Cost + Annual Cost / i
Cash Flow
The stream of monetary values — both inflows (receipts, revenues) and outflows (costs, investments) — resulting from an engineering project over its life.
Compound Amount Factor
Converts a present sum P to a future sum F at compound interest. The basic time-value factor.
(F/P, i%, n) = (1+i)ⁿ
Compound Interest
Interest calculated on both the original principal AND all previously accumulated interest. Produces exponential growth.
F = P(1+i)ⁿ
Contribution Margin
The selling price minus variable cost per unit. Each unit sold contributes this amount toward covering fixed costs and generating profit.
C = p − v
Declining Balance (DB)
An accelerated depreciation method where annual depreciation equals a fixed percentage of the book value at the start of each year.
BV_k = P(1−d%)ᵏ
Deferred Annuity
An annuity whose first payment is delayed until a future period D (rather than beginning at period 1).
P = A(P/A, i%, n−D+1)(P/F, i%, D−1)
Depletion
Reduction in value of natural resources (mines, oil wells, timber stands, quarries) due to gradual extraction of their contents. Analogous to depreciation for manufactured assets.
Depreciation
Reduction in the monetary value of an asset due to use, wear and tear, and passage of time. Recorded as a periodic expense to allocate the asset's cost over its useful life.
Discount
The difference between the future (maturity) value and the present value of a financial obligation. Also the process of finding present worth by discounting future values.
Effective Interest Rate
The true annual interest rate that accounts for the actual compounding frequency. Always greater than or equal to the nominal rate.
i_eff = (1 + r/m)ᵐ − 1
Engineering Economics
The application of economic analysis techniques to engineering decisions — selecting from alternatives to minimise costs and/or maximise benefits.
Equivalence
Different sums of money at different times that have the same economic value at a specified interest rate. The foundation for comparing alternatives.
Fixed Costs
Business costs that do not change with the level of production output. Examples: rent, depreciation, research and development, administrative overhead.
Future Worth (F)
The equivalent value of a present sum or cash flow series at some future time n, compounded at rate i.
F = P(1+i)ⁿ
Gradient Series (G)
A cash flow series where payments increase (or decrease) by a constant amount G each period. Period 1 payment = 0; period k payment = (k−1)G.
Interest
The money paid for the use of borrowed capital, or the return earned on invested capital over a given time period.
IRR (Internal Rate of Return)
The interest rate at which the present worth of all project cash flows equals zero. The project's actual earning rate.
Marginal Cost
The change in total cost resulting from producing one additional unit. Equals the variable cost per unit in the linear cost model.
Margin of Safety
The excess of actual (or budgeted) output over the break-even output. Measures how far sales can fall before losses begin.
MoS% = (Q_actual − Q_BEP)/Q_actual × 100
MARR
Minimum Acceptable Rate of Return. The threshold interest rate set by an organisation below which investments are rejected. Also called the hurdle rate.
Nominal Interest Rate
The stated annual interest rate that does not account for intra-year compounding. Must be converted to the effective rate for accurate calculations when compounding is more frequent than annual.
Perpetuity
An annuity that continues indefinitely, with no fixed end date. Its present worth equals A/i.
P = A/i
Present Worth (P)
The equivalent current value of a future cash flow or series, found by discounting at interest rate i. Also called present value.
P = F/(1+i)ⁿ
Profit/Volume Ratio (ϕ)
The ratio of contribution to sales revenue. Indicates what fraction of each naira of revenue contributes to covering fixed costs and generating profit.
ϕ = (p−v)/p = Contribution/Sales Revenue
Salvage Value (S)
The estimated resale, scrap, or disposal value of an asset at the end of its useful economic life. A positive cash inflow at year n in depreciation analysis.
Simple Interest
Interest calculated only on the original principal, not on previously accumulated interest. Produces linear growth.
F = P(1 + n·i)
Sinking Fund Factor
Converts a future sum F into the uniform annual payment A needed to accumulate that amount. Used for asset replacement planning.
(A/F, i%, n) = i/[(1+i)ⁿ−1]
Straight-Line Depreciation (SL)
The simplest depreciation method. Equal annual charges spread the depreciable cost evenly over the asset's useful life.
d = (P−S)/n per year
Sum-of-Years Digits (SYD)
An accelerated depreciation method that front-loads depreciation. Higher charges in early years, tapering in later years.
d_k = (n−k+1)/SOYD × (P−S); SOYD = n(n+1)/2
Time Value of Money (TVM)
The principle that money available now is worth more than the same amount in the future, due to its earning potential, inflation, and risk. The foundational concept of engineering economics.
Variable Costs
Costs that vary directly and proportionally with the level of production output. Examples: raw materials, direct labour, packaging, commissions, royalties per unit sold.
